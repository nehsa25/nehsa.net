    Affect/Effect
    Affect is a verb meaning to influence. The effect is a noun meaning result.

    Example:
    An exotic yellow bird landed on the branch and shrieked in contented happiness.

    The large yellow bird's shriek, sudden and piercing, affected Gaston deeply; it started him and brought back traumatic memories of Pierre. The effect was Gaston's sharp claws going into Timothée's leg, then a yelp and a bellowed, "GASTON!".

    This yell scared the bird and caused it to fly away.

    Fin.
    Time/Timing
    • Time refers to a specific point or duration, which is appropriate when discussing a measurement.
    • Timing often implies the act of determining or controlling time.


    Example:
    Timothée's perfect timing allowed him to arrive at the restaurant just as Gaston was leaving, and so they missed each other this time. Drats!
    Compliment/Complement
    Compliment is a nice thing to say. Complement is something that completes or goes well with something else.

    Example:
    Tim's quick reflexes complemented Gaston's composure as they narrowly avoided a waterskiing mishap, and Gaston returned the compliment by crediting Tim's sharp eyes for their escape.
    Among/Amongst
    Although among is more preferred in American English and amongst is more common in British English, I still use amongst because it sounds better

    What I do (and you probably should not):
    • If referring to two things: Among
    • If referring to more than two things: Amongst


    Example:
    Amongst the many people at the pool, Tim and Gaston stood out. Among themselves, they snorkeled to the bottom of the pool.
    To Whom/To Whomever
    "To whom it may concern" is more gramatically correct than "To whomever it may concern," but both are impersonal and we should probably just use "Dear <TITLE>" if you don't have a specific name to refer to.

    Whomever is used as the object of a verb or preposition such as: "I will give the prize to whomever wins the game." or "Please hire whomever you think is qualified." Easiest way to remember: Whomever needs an action.
    Submit/Remit
    Submit is to present for consideration or judgement. Remit is to send money in payment or as a gift.

    Example:
    Timothée submitted his application to the university, and remitted the payment for the application fee.
    On to/Onto
    Onto is used to indicate movement into or on a particular place or position. On to is used to indicate movement to a new topic or point.

    Example:
    Timothée stepped onto the stage, and then moved on to the next act.
