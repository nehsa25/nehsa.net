  • Semi-colons separate two independent clauses. "I am a writer; I am a reader." Refined folk use semi-colons for style points; in all cases, semi-colons could be periods if the clauses can stand alone.

    Pronoun
    A pronoun should refer a noun coming before the pronoun.
    Example: Mary is late for work. She is now sweaty.
    (Him/Her)
    Possessive Pronoun
    Example: Mary is late for work because she cannot find her keys.
    (His/Her)
    Antecedent (an·tuh·see·dnt)
    The noun being replaced by the pronoun. In the examples above, it'd be "Mary."

  • Confusing Words
    Affect/Effect
    Affect is a verb meaning to influence. The effect is a noun meaning result.

    Example:
    The bird shriek affected my concentration. The effect (impact) of the shriek was distraction.
    Time/Timing
    • Time refers to a specific point or duration, which is appropriate when discussing a measurement.
    • Timing often implies the act of determining or controlling time.


    Example:
    Timothée's perfect timing allowed him to arrive at the restaurant just as Gaston was leaving, and so they missed each other this time. Drats!
    Compliment/Complement
    Compliment is a nice thing to say. Complement is something that completes or goes well with something else.

    Example:
    Tim's quick reflexes complemented Gaston's composure as they narrowly avoided a waterskiing mishap, and Gaston returned the compliment by crediting Tim's sharp eyes for their escape.
    Among/Amongst
    Although among is more preferred in American English and amongst is more common in British English, I still use amongst because it sounds better

    What I do (and you probably should not):
    • If referring to two things: Among
    • If referring to more than two things: Amongst


    Example:
    Amongst the many people at the pool, Tim and Gaston stood out. Among themselves, they snorkeled to the bottom of the pool.
