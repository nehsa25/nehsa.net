Definitions

  • Radiation
    is any way in which energy is trasmitted through space from one point to another without the need for any physical connection between the two locations.
    Electromagnetic
    the energy is carried in the form of rapidly flucuating elecric and magnetic fields
    Visible Light
    electromagnetic radiation to which our eyes our sensative. As light enters our eye, small chemical reactions triggered by the incomming energy send electrical impulses to the brain. This produces the sensation of sight.
    Wave
    A way in which energy is transferred from place to place without the physical movement of material from one location to another.
    Wave Disturbance
    The cause that carries the energy. This distrubance, whatever it's nature, occurs in distinctive repeating pattern.
    Wave Period
    is the number of seconds needed for the wave to repeat itself at any given point in space.
    Wave Crest
    The apex of the wave
    Wave Trough
    The lowest point of the wave
    Wavelength λ
    is the number of meters needed for the wave to repeat itself at a given moment in time. It can be measured as the distance between two adjacent wave crests, two adjacent wave troughs, or any other similar point.
    Amptitude
    Maximum departure of the wave from the undisturbed state. E.g. A pond ripple would be from a flat surface (undistrubed water) to the crest.
    Frequency
    The number of wave crests passing any given point per unit of time is called the wave's frequency. A frequency of a wave is the reciprocal of the wave's period.

    Frequency is expressed in units of inverse time. i.e. 1/second (called hertz Hz). e.g. A wave with a period of 5 seconds has a frequency of 1/5 which is .2 Hz
    Visible Spectrum
    When passed through a prism, white light splits into its component colors. Spanning red-violet (in hues red, orange, yellow, green, blue, violet). The color is determined by the frequency. Light at different frequencies is split differently, red is altered the least, violet the most, creating the color spectrum.
    Electron or proton
    Elementary particles of atoms and all matter (the building blocks of everything). Erectrons are said to carry a negative charge, whereas protons have a positive charge. Electrical charge - A buildup of electrical charge (a net excess of positive over negative or vise versa) is what causes static cling and static shock

Laws

  • Kepler's laws
    1. Planet orbits are ellipses with the sun at one focus of the ellipse.
    2. A line joining a planet to the sun sweeps out equal areas in the ellipse over equal times.
    3. A planet's orbital period is proportional to the size of its orbit (its semi-major axis)
  • Newton's laws
    1. An object at rest stays at rest. An object in motion stays in motion at a constant speed in a straight line unless acted upon by an unbalanced force.
    2. The net force on an object is equivalent to the product of object's mass and its acceleration.
    3. For every force, there exists and equal and opposite force.

  • General/Special Relativity
    • The effects of special relativity are small unless the speed is high, a considerable fraction of the speed of light. The effects of general relativity are small unless there is a very dense massive object nearby.
    • Special theory of relativity
        1. The speed of light (c) is the maximum possible speed in the universe. E.g. in Newtonian law, if I'm riding a bike at 10mph and I throw a rock the same direction at 30mph, an observer would see the rock traveling at 40mph. I would see the rock traveling at 30mph. This isn't the same with speed of light, all observers would measure c (it cannot be faster). EVERYTHING IS c!
        2. There is no absolute frame of reference in the universe. That is, there is no "preferred" observer relative to whom all other velocities can be measured. Put another way, this is no way to tell who is moving and who is not. Instead, only relative velocities between observers matter (hense the term "relativity").
        3. Neither space nor time can be consider independently of one another. Rhather, they are components of a single entity: spacetime. There is no absolute, universal time.

Night Sky Journal

Journal Entry Instructions For each journal entry, include:
  1. The date and time
  2. Location: What is your longitude and latitude?
  3. Seeing conditions: How clear is the sky? Is it foggy, partly cloudy, etc. Are you in the city or a rural setting? What is the light pollution?
  4. Locate the Big Dipper and North Star. First, get oriented with the night sky. Figure out which direction is north, by finding the Big Dipper. The “front” two stars of the Big Dipper point to the star at the end of the handle of the Little Dipper, which is Polaris, or the North Star. The Big Dipper rotates around the celestial north pole through the night, but Polaris is always in the direction of north. Make a note of the orientation of the Big Dipper in the sky. Measure how high Polaris is in the sky. A convenient way to do this is to hold out your fist at arm’s length and count how many “fists” lie between the horizon and Polaris. Each fist is about ten degrees 90 degrees should be straight up. (If you live in the southern hemisphere, you will orient your viewing similarly, but with respect to the Southern Cross.)
  5. Choose a constellation to follow through the term. Find the constellation on your star finder, and then locate your chosen constellation in the night sky. Do this each night that you observe the sky. Note the location and orientation of the constellation by reporting its direction and inclination angle. Since you have located north, you should be able to approximate the direction (NNE, NE, ENE, etc.) and how many degrees above the horizon (inclination angle) your constellation is located.
  6. Locate a second constellation (a different one each time). Similarly report its location and orientation.
  7. Is the moon visible? What is its phase? Report its orientation and location.
  8. Give any other details. For example, is the Milky Way visible? What planets can you locate? Do you see any satellites? Meteor showers?
