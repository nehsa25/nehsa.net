Game Theory

 Game theory is the study of strategic interaction among rational decision-makers.
August 29th, 2024
Jesse Stone

I have been reading up on game theory and find the correlations to real life fascinating.

One study I read about by conducted by Robert Axelrod in 1980 involved inviting game theorists to submit a computer program that would play the Prisoner's Dilemma against each other.

Prisoner's Dilemma
Two players each have two options: Cooperate (C) or Defect (D) with the following consequences:
If both players cooperate, they each get 3 points.
If both players defect, they each get 1 point.
If one player cooperates and the other defects, the defector gets 5 points and the cooperator gets 0 points.

e.g.
Scenario Player 1 Result Player 2 Result
Both Cooperate 3 3
Player 1 Cooperates, Player 2 Defects 0 5
Player 1 Defects, Player 2 Cooperates 5 0
Both Defect 1 1


The goal is to maximize your own points.

The interest thing to me about this game is how the best strategy can change depending on how many rounds you play.

  • If you play a single game of Prisoner's Dilemma, the best strategy is to defect. This is because you will win (5 points) or draw (1 point). If you choose to cooperate, you may get nothing if the other player defects.
  • If you play a known number of rounds such as 10 rounds, the best strategy is to cooperate until the last round and then defect. The reasoning is that if you defect early, the other player will likely defect as well, and you will both lose. If you cooperate, you will both win, but knowing when the game will end, you can backstab and win! (5 points).
  • The most exciting scenario is an indefinite number of rounds- here, the best strategy is to cooperate. It's not just about winning a single round, it's about setting yourself up for success in the long run. If you defect, the other player will likely defect as well, and you will both lose. But if you cooperate, you will both win. This is the best strategy for the long term, ensuring mutual benefits and a positive outcome.

Key takeways:

  • Find win-wins, zero-sum games are not the only option (for me to win, you must lose)
  • Qualities for success:
    • Be nice (don't be first to defect)
    • Be retaliatory (don't be a push over - if someone defects, you should defect next round)
    • Be forgiving (don't continue to defect i.e. hold grudges)
  • "Nice" strategies are the most successful in the long run

Definitions:

Game Theory
Study of strategic interaction among rational decision-makers.
Zero-sum game
One's gain is another's loss. i.e. the "net" of the game is zero. A "Win-Win" is an eample of a non-zero-game as both "players" can win (net = 2).
