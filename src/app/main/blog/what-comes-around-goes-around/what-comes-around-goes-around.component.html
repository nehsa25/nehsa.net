Agism
August 29th, 2024
Jesse Stone

I was talking to a young software developer who said in passing (he didn't intend to be unkind), "Ohh, I didn't know people used <LANGUANGE> any longer."

Now, I do not believe that the language in question is going away or becoming obsolete (C#). That statement was just due to limited experience or a narrow worldview, but regardless, it wasn't very kind.

I recall an interview I gave over a decade two decades ago as Senior QA Manager, Strategy and Practice, for Webtrends. This older gentleman was interviewing for a testing position. He didn't do well. He only knew a few of the tools and technologies we used and only some standard terms I felt he should. My twenty-something mind told me this Senior Quality Engineer, with decades of experience, should have been a stronger candidate. We passed on this candidate and, very likely, hired someone in their early twenties straight out of college with no experience.

Back to the young developer I talked to recently, I thought, "Give it some time and see what you do today. What even may be considered an industry best practice, tool, or technology that will be outdated in the short future."

I think more and more about these people whose experiences I could have learned immensely from. I wish I had focused more on their experience and knowledge and less on their expertise in specific technologies of the day.

While I acknowledge that software development requires a lifelong passion for education, engineers who become complacent will be left behind. I now realize I focused on the wrong thing as a hiring manager. Tools and technologies change. Requiring years of experience in a niche technology or language is less critical than finding someone with the expertise to think and approach things correctly and with discipline.

As I get older, I think more about ageism (I know, duh). It's scary to think you could be left behind after dedicating your life and passions to an industry. AI exacerbates the fear.
