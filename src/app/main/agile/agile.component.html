I've spent much of my professional career in conversations, meetups, and conferences about Agile and project management. The project management processes are as important as the coding itself to ensure we're building the right thing and at the highest level of quality.

I have assisted several organizations in moving from waterfall processes to Agile development practices or at least to more iterative development patterns. Often, I see organizations spend a long time in a hybrid "mini-waterfall" state. The desire is to "be Agile," but there's a fear of drastically changing from known procedures, so we end up in a hybrid state. We're releasing more frequently, but the structure of the teams and processes remain much the same. Hence, we have the same problems and deficits in quality.

I make the following delineation:
Waterfall
Linear development of a large set of features. The features are designed, developed, and then tested, and only when everything is ready does a "release" occur.
Mini-waterfall
Is essentially the same as a waterfall, but with more frequent releases (i.e., perhaps quarterly instead of yearly). The development process remains linear, and teams are still segregated. This distinction helps to clarify the nuances between the two styles, ensuring a deeper understanding of the iterative development landscape. We're not utiliziing all the processes that allow us to "be Agile."
Iterative/Agile
Only a few key elements are needed for a successful iterative development:
  • Self-contained teamsâ€”One critical element for successful iterative development is the team's structure. When team members are split across projects, the quality and velocity of work suffer. For instance, expecting a tester to deliver thorough validation and find bugs at the end of a sprint when their focus has been elsewhere or they were not included in elaboration is a recipe for disappointment and subpar outcomes.
  • Features are broken up into user stories. "As a portal user, I need to download X from Y." This allows development to focus solely on fulfilling this requirement, and once tested, the story can be released in isolation and provide value to the end user.
  • Stories are sized via story points, not development hours. The reason: While development hours and story points may seem similar (I've been at organizations that insisted on a 1 story point = 4 development hours silliness, for example). This shows a misunderstanding of the purpose of story points.

    Development hours are provided by a developer with detailed knowledge of the underlying code. He imagines the code in his head and goes, "Well, Bob, I believe that's about 4 hours of development."

    All is well and good, except how much testing is then needed? Does it require a lot of documentation? Training? A developer coding task may be minor but have huge testing implications.

    Story points have a new name because they're intended to differ from development hours. They focus on relative complexity, and require no knowledge of the code itself. All members of the team may not be able to speak to the development hours of a feature written in a complex coding language, but if the team as a whole worked on user story "A" and is now tasked to develop user story "B," they can make some correlations:

    Well, if user story "A" was 3 points, story "B" would be 5 - more data validation is required, and we'll need to train the support team. Now, regardless of their knowledge of the actual code, every team member can participate in planning. The number we come up with is the "DONE DONE" number - the story has been developed and tested!

    Story points get a bad rap. For example, I see the memes of using 20-sided dice to determine story points. Still, when used correctly, they can help the team reach common ground for discussing the complexity of a feature.
  • Stories are prioritized in a groomed backlog. We take from the top for each release (sprint) until the sprint is full. If the team is ahead of schedule, they can pull in additional stories during the sprint; if behind schedule, anything not complete at the end of the sprint goes back into the backlog to be prioritized again.
  • Stories, once started are completed before moving on
  • Burn down is key. This is a burn down of story points within a sprint and is the best indication if changes are required to make deadlines with high quality. As stories are worked to completion and "DONE DONE" when complete, we can subtract their story points from the total allocated from teh sprint and have a perfect indication if we're on track. Do not split test tasks from development tasks. It's solely a means to trick ourselves into thinking we're further along than we are. Features are complete when they've been tested. At best splitting testing makes metrics *appear* better, at worst, it causes less testing to occur as once testing tasks are broken out from the feature, it's easy to de-prioritize them for new feature work.

Root Cause Analysis (RCA)

All organizations should consider an RCA process. These processes can be done after a major issue or escalation and allows for a structured approach to understanding what happened and how to prevent it in the future.
  • What Happened?
  • Why did it happen?
  • What would have caught the issue?
  • How was the issue addressed?
  • How do we prevent it in the future?
